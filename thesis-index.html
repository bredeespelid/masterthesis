Can foundation models beat a random walk at forecasting EUR/NOK?

We test whether transformer-based foundation models — Google's TimesFM and Amazon's Chronos2 — can outperform a driftless random walk and traditional econometric and machine-learning benchmarks on the EUR/NOK exchange rate at one- and three-month horizons.

Research questions

Three questions, one currency pair.

We frame the study around three connected questions: how foundation models stack up against the standard benchmarks, how the macro-financial information set changes the answer, and whether learning across other currencies improves EUR/NOK forecasts.

RQ 01

Foundation models vs. classical benchmarks

How do TimesFM 2.0/2.5 and Chronos2 compare to a driftless random walk, VAR/VARX, and gradient-boosted trees on EUR/NOK at one- and three-month horizons?

RQ 02

The information set

Does the choice of macro-financial covariates — VIX, equity indices, oil, real exchange rate, inflation and rate differentials — change which model wins, and by how much?

RQ 03

Cross-currency transfer

Can finetuning on a panel of other currencies (AUD, CAD, CHF, GBP, JPY, SEK, USD, …) before EUR/NOK evaluation produce more robust forecasts?

Model lineup

From a random walk to a finetuned transformer.

We benchmark four model families, then probe each foundation model with covariate ablations (LOFO) and cross-currency finetuning to see which design choices matter.

Key findings

Two-speed predictability — and why it matters.

The most consistent message: 1-month EUR/NOK is essentially noise, but 3-month movements contain enough persistent structure for the right foundation model — given the right covariates — to beat a random walk significantly.

The random walk is genuinely hard to beat at one month.

Across all 14 model variants, only two challengers beat the driftless random walk on RMSE at the 1-month horizon — and only by a hair. Directional accuracy of 71% for the random walk sets a high bar.

RW 1M RMSE 0.1394 RW directional 71% TimesFM 2.5 RMSE 0.1402 · DM p=0.0045

VAR and VARX collapse out of sample.

Fixed-parameter linear macro relations fall apart in the post-2020 regime. Quarterly RMSE balloons above 0.30 versus the random walk's 0.2127 — a clear case of structural-break risk in classical econometric models.

VAR/VARX 3M RMSE > 0.30 RW 3M RMSE 0.2127

Chronos2 + transfer learning + LOFO covariates wins quarterly.

The strongest 3-month model is Chronos2 finetuned on the cross-currency panel and conditioned on a compact covariate set — VIX, S&P 500, and the real exchange rate. The improvement is statistically significant by Diebold–Mariano.

Chronos2-FT-LOFO 3M RMSE 0.1971 vs. RW 0.2127 DM p-value 0.0230

The information set matters more than model size.

Price-only inputs and broad-macro kitchen-sink models both underperform. What works is a small, economically motivated trio: a global risk indicator (VIX), an equity-market signal (S&P 500), and a competitiveness anchor (real exchange rate q).

Best signals: VIX · S&P 500 · q

Cross-currency transfer gives Chronos2 robustness — but TimesFM 2.5 generalises broadly out of the box.

Finetuning Chronos2 on 11 other currencies before evaluating EUR/NOK improves quarterly performance, but the same recipe is weaker when the covariate set is restricted. TimesFM 2.5 gives the most consistent zero-shot performance across the FX panel.

Magnitude is modest but systematic.

The best models cut quarterly RMSE by about 0.015–0.02 units relative to the random walk — roughly 1.5–2 øre per euro at the 3-month horizon. Small in absolute terms, but reliable enough to matter for hedging and budgeting decisions.

Practical implications

So what?

Different decisions live on different time horizons. The findings translate directly into how each user of EUR/NOK forecasts should think about model choice and risk.

Norges Bank

Treat 1-month as noise.

Short-horizon EUR/NOK movements should be treated as essentially unpredictable. Medium-term projections benefit from conditioning on global risk indices, equity markets, and real competitiveness — not broad macro kitchen sinks.

Exporters / importers

Hedge on quarterly signal, not monthly.

Quarter-ahead hedging and budgeting can lean on foundation-model forecasts conditioned on a small, economically motivated covariate set. One-month decisions are better served by the random walk and explicit scenario analysis.

Forecasting practitioners

Pair foundation models with economics.

Foundation models earn their keep when paired with (a) a compact, economically motivated covariate set and (b) cross-currency finetuning. They are not drop-in replacements for traditional models, but they shift the frontier when used carefully.
